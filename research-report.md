# Build This: An Autonomous AI Incident-Response Agent (AI SRE) ‚Äî End-to-End Recommendation

## TL;DR
- **Build a read-only, glass-box "AI SRE" agent** orchestrated in **LangGraph** that ingests a Prometheus/Alertmanager webhook, runs *parallel* investigation checks (commit/deploy correlation, runbook RAG, impact estimation), posts a confidence-scored incident brief to Slack, and auto-drafts a blameless postmortem ‚Äî all driven against the **OpenTelemetry Astronomy Shop demo** with `flagd` fault injection as your synthetic production environment.
- **The differentiator is not the agent, it's the evaluation harness around it.** Ship a benchmark of 20‚Äì40 synthetic incidents with known root causes, measure precision/recall on root-cause identification and end-to-end latency, and record every agent decision with LangSmith/OpenTelemetry traces. Per IBM Research's ITBench paper (arXiv 2502.05352, ICML 2025), agents powered by state-of-the-art models "resolve only 13.8% of SRE scenarios," so a modest, *honestly measured* accuracy number on a scoped benchmark reads as senior-level maturity, not weakness.
- **Do NOT do automated remediation.** Read-only/advisory (detect ‚Üí investigate ‚Üí correlate ‚Üí brief ‚Üí postmortem) is the safest scope that is still genuinely impressive; propose remediation as a "would-do-next" with human approval gates. Total build: an end-to-end vertical slice in week 1, MVP by week 3‚Äì4, polish and eval harness by week 6‚Äì8.

## Key Findings

### The concept is validated by the market ‚Äî which is both good and dangerous
Autonomous incident response is exactly what the 2025‚Äì2026 AIOps/AI-SRE market is racing toward: incident.io, Rootly, PagerDuty AIOps, FireHydrant, Datadog Bits AI, AWS DevOps Agent, Resolve.ai, and open-source tools all do versions of this. Gartner's *Market Guide for AI Site Reliability Engineering Tooling* (by Daniel Betts, Chris Saunderson and Hassan Ennaciri, published 26 January 2026) predicts "By 2029, 85% of enterprises will use AI SRE tooling... up from less than 5% in 2025." This means your project is *legible* to any infra interviewer ‚Äî they will immediately understand what it is and why it matters. It also means you must execute at a level that survives comparison to products they may already use. The winning move is to copy the *architecture patterns* of the best tools while being honest that you're building a scoped, demoable slice. (Note: a credible open-source analog to study is **Aurora by Arvo AI** ‚Äî "a LangGraph-orchestrated ReAct agent, 30+ integrations across AWS, Azure, GCP, OVH, Scaleway, and Kubernetes," Apache 2.0, at github.com/Arvo-AI/aurora ‚Äî which validates that your exact stack choice is production-shaped.)

### What the best tools actually do (study these patterns)
incident.io's AI SRE runs a **multi-agent "investigation"** that spawns the moment an incident is declared. Per founding engineer Lawrence Jones and AI product lead Ed Dean, it runs **parallel "checks"** across GitHub/GitLab code changes, historical incidents, Slack, and observability data; **gates** the investigation until it has enough context (to avoid the LLM's "positivity bias" of generating confident nonsense from thin inputs); forms a **hypothesis, then tests it** by reading code and querying dashboards; and posts a root cause with evidence and next steps to Slack within 1‚Äì2 minutes. A crucial, counterintuitive lesson from that team, stated on the *Just Now Possible* podcast (6 Nov 2025): **"Retrieval-augmented reasoning still benefits from simplicity: deterministic tagging and re-ranking often beat complex vector setups. Post-incident 'time travel' evals let teams score AI accuracy after they know what really happened."**

Rootly and Resolve.ai describe the same shape: **parallel hypothesis checks, confidence scores, and a visible reasoning chain** ("Payment latency likely caused by Catalog deploy at 14:03 UTC (confidence 0.74)"). The universal design principle worth stealing is the **"glass box vs. black box"** distinction: a black-box AI says "the root cause is a memory leak"; a glass-box AI says "based on this log line at 14:31 UTC showing 98% memory, correlated with this PR, here's my hypothesis (confidence 0.74)." **Every finding cites its evidence.** That single design choice is what separates an impressive agent from a chatbot.

### The reality check that makes your project credible
Independent benchmarks show autonomous incident RCA is genuinely *hard*. On IBM's ITBench (an ICML 2025 spotlight paper, arXiv 2502.05352), across the 42 SRE scenarios in the 94-scenario release, "agents powered by state-of-the-art models resolve only 13.8% of SRE scenarios, 25.2% of CISO scenarios, and 0% of FinOps scenarios" (a revised 102-scenario version reports 11.4% on SRE). On the independent ITBench-AA implementation (59 Kubernetes tasks: 40 public + 19 private), **all frontier models scored below 50%**, making it one of the least-saturated agentic benchmarks. A sharp insight from that leaderboard: *more investigation turns did not mean better answers* ‚Äî over-investigating agents surface false positives. Datadog's engineering team building Bits AI SRE found that "improvements in one area could quietly introduce regressions in another," which is precisely why they built a replayable evaluation platform with ground-truth root causes and world-snapshots. **You should cite these numbers in your README.** Framing your own honest metrics against the state of the art is exactly the systems-thinking maturity a staff reviewer wants.

## Details

### ARCHITECTURE AND SCOPE

**Scope decision: read-only/advisory, glass-box, with remediation as a designed-but-gated capability.** The safest option that is still genuinely impressive is detect ‚Üí investigate ‚Üí correlate ‚Üí brief ‚Üí postmortem, with **zero autonomous mutating actions**. This is not a cop-out ‚Äî it mirrors what incident.io and Datadog actually ship, because the substantial majority of AI-driven ops decisions still require human verification. To avoid reading as timid, *design* the remediation interface (a `propose_remediation` tool that emits a structured rollback/restart/scale recommendation with a required human-approval step and a dry-run mode) but never wire it to real actions. This demonstrates you understand blast radius, guardrails, and autonomy-vs-control tradeoffs ‚Äî a top interview talking point ‚Äî without the risk of an embarrassing live demo where the agent nukes your demo environment.

**System architecture (sketch-ready):**

1. **Ingestion layer** ‚Äî A FastAPI service exposes `/webhook/alert`. Prometheus Alertmanager is configured with a `webhook_configs` receiver pointing at it. The endpoint validates the Alertmanager payload (JSON: `status`, `alerts[]`, `labels`, `annotations`, `startsAt`), returns 200 immediately, and enqueues an investigation (Redis-backed queue ‚Äî plays to your existing Redis skill). This decoupling (ingest fast, process async) is the same pattern Slack's own Events API docs recommend.
2. **Orchestrator (LangGraph state machine)** ‚Äî A typed `InvestigationState` graph. Nodes: `triage` (parse alert, classify severity/type) ‚Üí `gate` (do we have enough context? if not, gather more) ‚Üí **fan-out to parallel investigator nodes** ‚Üí `synthesize` (rank hypotheses by confidence + evidence) ‚Üí `brief` (post to Slack) ‚Üí later, on resolution signal ‚Üí `postmortem`.
3. **Parallel investigator nodes (the "tools"):**
   - **Commit/deploy correlator** ‚Äî queries a seeded Git repo + a deploy-history table (SQLite/Postgres), correlates alert `startsAt` with deploy timestamps, runs `git log`/`git diff` on the suspect window, and passes candidate diffs to the LLM for reasoning.
   - **Runbook retriever** ‚Äî RAG over a curated runbook corpus (pgvector).
   - **Impact estimator** ‚Äî queries Prometheus for error-rate/latency deltas and the service dependency graph to compute blast radius (services + endpoints + estimated affected users).
   - **Similar-incident matcher** ‚Äî embedding search over past (synthetic) postmortems.
4. **State store** ‚Äî Postgres (with pgvector) holds investigations, findings, hypotheses, evidence links, and postmortems. Redis for the work queue and hot state.
5. **Slack integration** ‚Äî A Slack Bolt app posts a Block Kit incident brief; slash commands (`/incident status`, `/incident postmortem`) allow interaction.
6. **Observability** ‚Äî LangSmith (or self-hosted Langfuse) + OpenTelemetry traces on every node, tool call, and LLM call.

**What makes it read as genuinely agentic (not prompt chaining):** The defining property is **autonomous, evidence-driven control flow** ‚Äî the agent *decides* which tools to call, in what order, and when it has enough evidence to stop, based on observations (the ReAct thought‚Üíaction‚Üíobservation loop, plus a gating node). Concretely, four choices signal real agency to a staff reviewer: (1) the **gate node** that refuses to conclude on thin evidence (mirrors incident.io's anti-positivity-bias gating); (2) **parallel hypothesis generation with confidence scoring and explicit invalidation** ‚Äî the agent marks each hypothesis validated/invalidated/inconclusive with evidence, like a senior engineer; (3) **a generator/evaluator split** ‚Äî a separate "critic" step scores the brief before posting, because (per Anthropic's agent-evaluation guidance) agents skew positive when grading their own work; (4) **every conclusion cites the specific log line / PR / metric it rests on** (glass box). Prompt chaining is a fixed DAG of LLM calls; your system has conditional edges, dynamic tool selection, a stopping criterion, and self-critique. Say exactly that in interviews.

### TECH STACK

**Agentic orchestration: use LangGraph. This is a real recommendation, not a hedge.** For this project specifically, LangGraph is correct because: (a) incident investigation is a **stateful, branching, partially-parallel workflow with a stopping criterion** ‚Äî exactly what graph-based orchestration is for, and past ~3 decision points graph frameworks beat imperative ones on maintainability; (b) it has **built-in checkpointing and time-travel debugging**, which doubles as a demo feature (you can replay an investigation step-by-step in an interview); (c) **first-class LangSmith tracing** gives you the step-by-step reasoning view for free; (d) it's the framework the actual AI-SRE tools use (Aurora is "LangGraph-orchestrated" with 30+ tools). LangChain's 22 Oct 2025 announcement confirms LangGraph 1.0 is "the first stable major release in the durable agent framework space," adopted by Uber, LinkedIn and Klarna, and per pypistats.org the `langgraph` package saw roughly 58 million downloads in the trailing month ‚Äî so it reads as a production-credible choice.

*Why not the others:* **CrewAI** ("crew of role-playing agents") is fastest to prototype but its role metaphor doesn't fit a single-investigation-with-parallel-checks workflow, and it lacks the checkpointing you want for demoing. **OpenAI Agents SDK** is the lowest-friction path but is OpenAI-model-locked and its handoff model makes branching/persistence awkward ‚Äî plus vendor lock-in is a weaker resume signal than a model-agnostic graph. **AutoGen/AG2** is oriented to conversational multi-agent debate, not tool-execution pipelines, and Microsoft has folded it into the Azure-centric Agent Framework. **Raw Anthropic/OpenAI function calling** is a legitimate "I understand the primitives" flex, and you *should* be able to explain that LangGraph is just orchestrating tool-use loops under the hood ‚Äî but hand-rolling state, retries, parallelism, and tracing is reinventing exactly what LangGraph gives you. Recommendation: **LangGraph + Anthropic Claude (Sonnet-class) as the reasoning model**, model-agnostic via LangGraph so you can swap. Use **Pydantic** for all structured tool I/O.

**Backend language: stay in Python/FastAPI, with one strategic Go component.** Your FastAPI/Redis/Polars skills are the right foundation and the agent ecosystem is Python-native ‚Äî fighting that costs you weeks. But you have an order-book project with sub-10¬µs p99 ambitions and an LSM-tree engine, so you clearly *can* do systems work, and infra interviewers love language range. The highest-leverage place to add Go is a small, standalone **synthetic telemetry / load generator** (or a metrics-correlation microservice) that produces realistic traffic and error patterns against the demo app and exposes them for the impact estimator. It's genuinely justified by performance (high-throughput event generation), it's cleanly decoupled, and it gives you a "why Go here, Python there" story. Do **not** rewrite the orchestrator in Go ‚Äî that's resume-diversity theater at the cost of velocity.

**Simulating the production environment ‚Äî this is the make-or-break setup.** Use the **OpenTelemetry Demo ("Astronomy Shop")**: 15+ polyglot microservices (Go, Python, .NET, Java, etc.) over gRPC/HTTP, fully instrumented, shipping with Prometheus, Grafana, Jaeger, and a **`flagd` feature-flag fault injector** plus a built-in load generator ‚Äî all via `docker compose up`. This is the single best-fit open-source foundation because it turns "simulate a production incident" into flipping a flag: `paymentServiceFailure`, `cartServiceFailure`, `productCatalogFailure`, `kafkaQueueProblems`, `adServiceHighCpu`, `imageSlowLoad`, `loadGeneratorFloodHomepage`, plus a memory-leak scenario. Each flag produces real, correlated telemetry (error spikes, latency, Kafka consumer lag) that your agent investigates for real. Layer on top:
- **Alerts:** Prometheus alerting rules on the demo's metrics ‚Üí Alertmanager `webhook` receiver ‚Üí your FastAPI endpoint. (Well-documented, ~10-line config.)
- **Commit/deploy history:** Fork the demo into a **seeded Git repo** where you author a scripted history of commits and "deploys," including deliberately bad commits that map to each fault flag (e.g., a commit that "introduces" the payment failure). This is what makes commit correlation demoable and *defensible* ‚Äî you have ground truth.
- **Runbooks:** Write 15‚Äì30 markdown runbooks (one per incident type + generic ones) as your RAG corpus.
- **Metrics/logs for impact:** query Prometheus directly + your Go load generator for traffic baselines.

**Identifying the "likely bad commit" ‚Äî use a combination, weighted toward timing correlation + LLM diff reasoning.** The most defensible and demoable approach is a two-stage funnel (this mirrors Meta's published RCA architecture, which reduces thousands of changes to a few hundred with heuristic retrieval, then applies an LLM ranker): **Stage 1 ‚Äî deterministic retrieval:** correlate alert `startsAt` against deploy timestamps to reduce many commits to the handful deployed in the suspect window; use `git log --since/--until` and `git log -S`/`-L` to find commits touching the implicated service/files; use CODEOWNERS/path heuristics. **Stage 2 ‚Äî LLM ranking:** feed the candidate diffs + commit messages to the model to rank likely culprits *with reasoning and a confidence score*. Pure `git blame` is too naive alone; pure LLM-over-all-commits doesn't scale and hallucinates; **timing correlation to shrink the search space, then LLM reasoning to rank, is exactly what the credible tools do** and is trivially demoable because your seeded history gives you a known-correct answer.

**Runbook retrieval: RAG over pgvector ‚Äî do NOT over-engineer the vector DB.** At your scale (dozens to low-hundreds of runbook chunks), the right choice is **pgvector on the Postgres you're already running**. The 2026 consensus is explicit: below tens of millions of vectors, a dedicated vector DB is operational overhead you don't need, and pgvector lets you join vectors against relational incident data in one system. Use an embedding model in the 384‚Äì1536 dim range (e.g., a BGE/GTE small model or OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small`), HNSW index, chunk runbooks by section. **Heed incident.io's lesson**: for a corpus this small and well-structured, deterministic tagging + re-ranking (tag runbooks by service/alert-type, retrieve by tag, then embed-rank within) often beats naive vector search ‚Äî implement hybrid retrieval and *say why* in your writeup. Chroma is a fine prototyping alternative but pgvector is the more defensible "I made one system do the job" story.

**Estimating impact credibly without real traffic.** Generate synthetic-but-realistic signals: (1) the demo's **load generator** produces a real baseline of requests/errors/latency; (2) your **Go traffic generator** can dial up realistic patterns (diurnal curves, per-endpoint mix); (3) the agent computes impact from **actual Prometheus deltas** during a fault ‚Äî error-rate increase, p99 latency change, and blast radius via the **service dependency graph** (which downstream services call the failing one). Report impact as: services affected (from topology), endpoints affected (from span/metric labels), estimated affected users (baseline request volume √ó error rate over incident duration), and a severity classification. Because the numbers derive from real telemetry the fault actually produced, the demo feels genuine rather than hand-waved.

**Slack integration: build a real Slack Bolt bot, not just an incoming webhook.** Incoming webhooks are one-way and channel-locked; they'd make the project look like a toy. A **Bolt app (Python)** using `chat.postMessage` gives you Block Kit rich formatting, threading (post updates as replies), dynamic channel targeting, and ‚Äî critically ‚Äî **slash commands and interactivity** (`/incident status`, buttons to request the postmortem, an "acknowledge" action). That interactivity is a live-demo highlight and demonstrates you understand the Events API 3-second-ack + async-processing pattern. Verify request signatures.

**Postmortem generation: structured LLM output into a fixed template, grounded in captured evidence.** Design: (1) Define a **Pydantic schema** matching the Google SRE / industry template ‚Äî `summary`, `timeline[]` (timestamp + event), `impact` (users/services/revenue/duration), `root_cause`, `contributing_factors[]`, `resolution`, `action_items[]` (owner + verifiable outcome + deadline), `lessons_learned`. (2) Use the model's **native structured-output / JSON-schema mode** with Pydantic validation ‚Äî put a `reasoning` field *before* conclusion fields so chain-of-thought is baked into generation order. (3) **Ground it in evidence you actually captured during the investigation** (the trace: alert time, correlated commit, Prometheus deltas, runbook used, resolution signal) ‚Äî the timeline is assembled from *hard timestamps in your event log*, not model memory. This is the #1 anti-"generic ChatGPT" technique: a postmortem built from real captured events with real links reads nothing like a hallucinated essay. Store as Markdown + the structured JSON (and render to a file/Notion-style page). Blameless framing ("what about the system allowed this," not "who").

**Observability/tracing on the agent itself ‚Äî this is a demo weapon.** Use **LangSmith** (native LangGraph integration, one env var, free tier) for the step-by-step trace view: every node transition, tool call, LLM prompt/response, latency, and token cost, rendered as an expandable execution tree. For a self-hosted/open-source story, use **Langfuse** (open-source, self-hostable) or emit **OpenTelemetry GenAI spans** (LangSmith now has full OTel support). Key insight to articulate: **agent observability needs *intent* visibility, not just execution visibility** ‚Äî log the agent's *reasoning* at each decision fork, not just the tool outputs, so you can distinguish "the agent chose wrong" from "the agent got bad inputs." In an interview, pulling up a LangSmith trace showing the agent gating, fanning out, forming and invalidating hypotheses, and citing evidence is the moment that proves it's a real system.

### IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

**Build order ‚Äî smallest end-to-end vertical slice first, then deepen.** The cardinal rule is: get one alert ‚Üí one investigation ‚Üí one Slack message working end-to-end before adding any depth.

- **Week 1 ‚Äî Walking skeleton (the vertical slice).** Stand up the OTel demo via docker-compose. Wire Prometheus alerting rule ‚Üí Alertmanager webhook ‚Üí FastAPI endpoint. Build a *trivial* LangGraph graph: one node that reads the alert and posts a hardcoded-format message to Slack via Bolt. **Milestone: flip a `flagd` fault flag, watch an alert fire, and see a Slack message appear.** This de-risks the entire integration surface early.
- **Week 2 ‚Äî Commit correlation + real triage.** Seed the Git repo with scripted commit/deploy history mapped to fault flags. Build the deploy-timing correlator + `git log` analysis + LLM diff ranking node. The Slack brief now names a suspect commit with confidence.
- **Week 3 ‚Äî Runbook RAG + impact estimation ‚Üí MVP.** Add pgvector runbook retrieval and the Prometheus-based impact estimator with dependency-graph blast radius. **This is the true MVP** (see below).
- **Week 4 ‚Äî Postmortem generation.** Capture the event log during investigation; on a resolution signal (fault flag flipped off / recovery detected), generate the structured postmortem from captured evidence.
- **Weeks 5‚Äì6 ‚Äî The evaluation harness (highest-value addition).** Build 20‚Äì40 scripted synthetic incidents with known root causes; run the agent headless against each; score precision/recall on root-cause and bad-commit identification, runbook-retrieval hit rate, and end-to-end latency. Add the generator/evaluator critic step.
- **Weeks 7‚Äì8 ‚Äî Polish for demo + differentiation.** LangSmith trace polish, Slack Block Kit refinement, README with architecture diagram + honest benchmark results + the ITBench context, a 2‚Äì3 minute recorded demo video, and the designed-but-gated remediation interface.

**True MVP definition:** *A fired alert (from a real `flagd` fault) triggers an autonomous LangGraph investigation that (1) correlates the alert to the likely bad commit with a confidence score and evidence link, (2) retrieves the most relevant runbook, (3) estimates impact (services/endpoints/users) from real telemetry, and (4) posts a single well-formatted, evidence-cited incident brief to Slack ‚Äî end to end, no human in the loop.* That is the smallest version that fully demonstrates the core concept. Postmortem and eval harness are additive.

**Highest-impact additions after MVP, ranked by impact-per-effort:**
1. **Evaluation harness with precision/recall + latency** (moderate effort, *highest* impact ‚Äî this is the single thing that separates you from the forgettable pile).
2. **LangSmith trace view polished for demo** (low effort, high impact ‚Äî it's your proof of "real system").
3. **Auto-postmortem from captured evidence** (moderate effort, high impact ‚Äî completes the narrative arc).
4. **Generator/evaluator critic step** (low effort, medium-high impact ‚Äî sophisticated design signal).
5. **Designed-but-gated remediation interface** (low effort, medium impact ‚Äî great interview talking point).
6. **Go synthetic-traffic component** (medium effort, medium impact ‚Äî resume language diversity).
7. **Hybrid deterministic+vector runbook retrieval** (low-medium effort, medium impact ‚Äî shows retrieval sophistication).

**Hardest technical parts & where you'll get stuck:**
- **Commit correlation precision.** Naive timing correlation over-matches when multiple commits deploy together. *Approach:* keep the seeded history realistic but tractable; use the two-stage funnel; report confidence, not certainty; let the eval harness quantify precision/recall so you can tune.
- **The gating/stopping criterion (avoiding runaway loops).** Agents get stuck re-investigating or over-investigating (the ITBench-AA "more turns ‚âÝ better" finding). *Approach:* hard cap on investigation steps, an explicit "sufficient evidence" gate, and timeouts on every tool.
- **Impact estimation feeling real.** *Approach:* derive everything from actual Prometheus deltas + dependency topology; never let the LLM invent numbers ‚Äî it only *narrates* numbers your code computed.
- **Grounding the postmortem (anti-hallucination).** *Approach:* assemble the timeline from your hard event log; the LLM formats, it doesn't recall.
- **Non-determinism in the demo/evals.** Live telemetry races with faults. *Approach:* for the eval harness, capture "world snapshots" (like Datadog) so scoring is reproducible; for the live demo, pre-warm a known-good fault scenario.
- **Slack 3-second ack.** *Approach:* ack immediately, process async off the Redis queue.

**Testing/evaluation approach (do this ‚Äî it's the crown jewel):** Build a benchmark set of 20‚Äì40 synthetic incidents, each a (fault-flag scenario + scripted bad commit + expected runbook + expected impact class) tuple with ground-truth labels. Run the agent headless per scenario, N repeats. Measure: **root-cause identification precision/recall**, **bad-commit top-1/top-3 accuracy**, **runbook retrieval hit rate**, **impact-classification accuracy**, and **end-to-end latency (p50/p95)**. Use post-hoc "time-travel" scoring (you know the true cause). Report the numbers honestly in the README against the ITBench context. Per Anthropic's agent-evaluation guidance, 20‚Äì50 tasks drawn from real failures is a great start ‚Äî you don't need hundreds.

**Why most agentic portfolio projects are forgettable, and how to not be.** The 2025‚Äì2026 interview cycle is flooded with "wrapped an LLM in a loop" projects. Per Anthropic's engineering guidance, **the eval harness is the differentiator** ‚Äî "20‚Äì50 simple tasks drawn from real failures is a great start," and teams that skip evals signal they ship unevaluated features (a disqualifier at serious companies). Hiring-side write-ups agree: tool use is *table stakes*; what impresses is **how you handle the hard parts** ‚Äî garbage inputs, loop prevention, and *measuring* whether the agent is right. Concretely, the forgettable projects: (a) have no evaluation, (b) are black boxes with no reasoning trace, (c) demo one happy path and break on edge cases, (d) do "sophistication" (5 agents) instead of reliability. Yours stands out by inverting all four: **rigorous eval harness with real metrics, glass-box evidence-cited reasoning, a polished trace view, restraint (read-only, does less but reliably), and honest limitations documented.** Senior reviewers consistently say they prefer an agent that "does less but does it reliably" and can be debugged over a flashy multi-agent demo that works "most of the time."

**Should it become a real open-source tool?** *Partially ‚Äî and strategically.* A fully-supported OSS product with real users is a large ongoing commitment that competes with polished existing tools and risks diluting your interview-prep time. **The right move: build the self-contained demo first (weeks 1‚Äì8), then open-source it cleanly** ‚Äî great README, architecture diagram, one-command `docker compose up` that stands up the whole thing (demo app + agent + Slack instructions), the eval harness runnable by anyone, and the benchmark results published. That gets you 90% of the credibility (a real, reproducible, runnable artifact reviewers can clone) for 10% of the cost of "maintaining a product." If it organically attracts GitHub stars or contributors, lean in ‚Äî a sustained commit history on an agent repo is itself a strong hiring signal. But **do not** gate your success on adoption; a polished, honestly-evaluated, reproducible demo is more than enough to get named in interviews.

## Recommendations

**Start here, in this order:**
1. **This week:** `docker compose up` the OpenTelemetry Astronomy Shop demo; confirm you can flip `flagd` fault flags and see telemetry change in Grafana. Create the seeded Git fork.
2. **Build the walking skeleton (week 1):** Alertmanager webhook ‚Üí FastAPI ‚Üí trivial LangGraph node ‚Üí Slack Bolt message. Ship the end-to-end path before any depth.

5. **Polish + open-source + record demo (weeks 7‚Äì8).**

**Decision thresholds that should change your plan:**
